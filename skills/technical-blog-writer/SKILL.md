# Technical Blog Writer

Write technical blog posts that are expert-level but accessible to non-developers. This skill combines deep research with clear, explanatory writing that makes complex topics understandable without dumbing them down.

## Our writing philosophy

We believe the best technical content comes from sharing our collective experience. When we write from our team's perspective—using "we" and "our"—we create more authentic and helpful content. Readers connect better with writing that reflects real experience rather than abstract instruction.

We've made a choice: our content will sound like it comes from experienced practitioners sharing what we've learned, not from a generic instruction manual. This approach builds trust and makes technical concepts more approachable.

### Natural team voice

We avoid repetitive patterns that sound artificial. Instead of overusing "we've" constructions, we vary how we express our experience:

**Instead of:** "We've found that... We've learned that... We've discovered that..."
**We use:** "Our tests showed... What we learned was... Here's what surprised us..."

**Key principles:**
- **Vary sentence starters**: Mix "we found," "our team discovered," "here's what worked"
- **Use direct statements**: Sometimes just state facts without "we" framing
- **Include specific details**: Replace generic claims with concrete examples
- **Vary sentence rhythm**: Mix short, direct statements with longer explanations
- **Sound natural**: Write like we're talking to colleagues, not following a formula

## When to use this skill

Use this skill when you need to:
- Write technical blog posts or articles
- Explain complex coding concepts to mixed audiences
- Create tutorials that bridge technical and non-technical readers
- Document engineering challenges and solutions
- Write about software architecture, development practices, or technical decisions

## Core approach

### 1. Research first, write second

Before writing anything, research the topic thoroughly:
- Search for current, authoritative sources on the topic
- Look for real-world examples and case studies
- Find recent developments or best practices
- Identify common challenges or misconceptions

### 2. Problem-solution structure

Always structure your content around:
- **The problem**: What challenge or question are you addressing?
- **The solution**: How do you solve it?
- **The implementation**: How does it work in practice?
- **The tradeoffs**: What are the pros and cons?

### 3. Write for smart non-experts

Assume your reader is intelligent but may not have your specific technical background:
- Explain jargon when you first use it
- Use analogies and examples to illustrate concepts
- Focus on "why" before "how"
- Include code only when it adds clarity

## Writing process

### Step 1: Research and outline

1. **Research the topic**: Use web search to find current, authoritative sources
2. **Identify the core problem**: What specific challenge are you solving?
3. **Map the solution**: What's the approach or answer?
4. **Create an outline**: Structure your article before writing

### Step 2: Draft the article

Follow this structure:

```markdown
# [Compelling, specific title]

## The problem
Start with the concrete challenge or question. Use a real example if possible.

## Why this matters
Explain the impact or importance. Who is affected? What are the consequences?

## The solution
Present your approach clearly. Focus on the core concept first.

## How it works
Walk through the implementation. Use code examples only when they clarify the concept.

## Tradeoffs and considerations
Discuss limitations, alternatives, or edge cases.

## Key takeaways
Summarize the main points in actionable terms.

## Frequently Asked Questions
Include 3-5 common questions readers might have about the topic.
```

### Step 3: Refine and polish

- **Check clarity**: Read from a non-expert perspective
- **Verify accuracy**: Ensure technical details are correct
- **Improve flow**: Make sure sections connect logically
- **Add examples**: Include concrete scenarios where helpful

## Writing style guidelines

### Tone and voice

- **Thoughtful**: We consider the broader context and implications of what we write
- **Concise**: We use simple, direct language. We avoid unnecessary words
- **Explanatory**: We focus on making concepts understandable through our experience
- **Practical**: We emphasize real-world application over theory, sharing what we've learned
- **Experienced**: We write from our team's perspective using "we" and "our"
- **Authentic**: We share our actual experiences, including challenges and decisions
- **Transparent**: We explain why we made certain choices and what we've discovered
- **Varied**: We mix different ways of expressing team experience to sound natural

### Voice variation patterns

**Alternative ways to express team experience:**
- "We've found that..." → "Our tests showed..." / "Here's what we discovered..."
- "We've learned that..." → "What we learned was..." / "The key insight for us was..."
- "We've seen..." → "In our experience..." / "We've encountered cases where..."
- "We've discovered..." → "What surprised us was..." / "We didn't expect..."

### Anti-patterns to avoid

**Repetitive constructions:**
- Don't start multiple sentences with the same "we've" pattern
- Avoid consistent sentence length and structure
- Don't overuse the same team voice phrases

**Artificial patterns:**
- "We've found that... We've learned that... We've discovered that..." (too repetitive)
- "We believe... We think... We feel..." (unnecessary qualification)
- Perfect, formulaic transitions between ideas

**Instead, sound natural:**
- Mix direct statements with team perspective
- Vary sentence length and rhythm
- Use specific details rather than generic claims
- Write like you're talking to colleagues

### Language choices

- Use plain English over technical jargon
- Explain necessary terms when first introduced
- Prefer active voice over passive voice
- Use short sentences and paragraphs
- Write "you" and "we" to create connection with reader

### Code examples

Only include code when it:
- Demonstrates a concept more clearly than words
- Shows a practical implementation
- Illustrates a specific pattern or technique

Format code with:
- Clear, descriptive variable names
- Comments explaining non-obvious parts
- Context about what the code accomplishes

## Research guidelines

### Source quality

Prioritize:
- Official documentation
- Well-regarded technical blogs
- Academic papers (when applicable)
- Recent articles (within last 2 years)
- Sources with real-world examples

### Research process

1. **Broad search**: Understand the landscape
2. **Deep dive**: Focus on specific aspects
3. **Cross-reference**: Verify claims across sources
4. **Current state**: Ensure information is up-to-date

## Common pitfalls to avoid

### Don't:

- Write overly technical explanations without context
- Assume prior knowledge without explanation
- Use code as a substitute for clear explanation
- Focus on implementation details without explaining the "why"
- Write generic content without specific examples

### Do:

- Start with concrete problems or questions
- Explain the significance before diving into details
- Use analogies and real-world examples
- Acknowledge limitations and tradeoffs
- Write for the intelligent non-expert

## FAQ section guidelines

### When to include FAQs

Always include an FAQ section when:
- The topic involves common misconceptions or confusion points
- Readers might have practical implementation questions
- There are "what if" scenarios or edge cases to address
- The topic is complex and benefits from clarification
- You want to address follow-up questions proactively

### How to identify good FAQ questions

Based on your article content, look for:
- **Common confusion points**: What concepts do people typically misunderstand?
- **Practical concerns**: How do readers actually implement this?
- **Edge cases**: What happens in unusual situations?
- **Prerequisites**: What do readers need to know first?
- **Alternatives**: What if this approach doesn't work for them?
- **Next steps**: What should readers do after reading?

### FAQ writing best practices

- **Be direct**: Answer questions concisely without unnecessary preamble
- **Stay focused**: Each FAQ should address one specific question
- **Reference the article**: Link back to relevant sections when helpful
- **Be practical**: Provide actionable guidance when possible
- **Avoid speculation**: Don't guess about uncertain topics
- **Keep it brief**: FAQ answers should be shorter than main content

### FAQ formatting

Use this consistent format:
```markdown
## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: [Clear, specific question]
A: [Direct, concise answer that addresses the question. Reference relevant sections if helpful.]

### Q: [Another common question]
A: [Clear answer with practical guidance or clarification.]
```

### FAQ integration tips

- Place FAQs at the end, after key takeaways
- Use questions that genuinely add value beyond the main content
- Avoid repeating information already covered in detail
- Keep the section to 3-5 questions maximum
- Review FAQs to ensure they complement rather than duplicate the article

## Example outputs

### Good technical blog post (with our voice):

```markdown
# Debugging race conditions in distributed systems

## The problem
Your API works perfectly in development but fails randomly in production. Requests that should succeed sometimes return errors, and you can't reproduce the issue consistently.

## Why this matters
Race conditions in distributed systems can cause data corruption, lost transactions, and frustrated users. They're especially dangerous because they're intermittent and hard to debug.

## The solution
Use distributed locks to ensure only one process can modify shared resources at a time. This prevents concurrent processes from interfering with each other.

## How it works

```python
import redis
import time

def with_lock(key, func, timeout=10):
    """Execute func with distributed lock"""
    r = redis.Redis()
    lock_key = f"lock:{key}"
    
    # Try to acquire lock
    if r.set(lock_key, "locked", nx=True, ex=timeout):
        try:
            return func()
        finally:
            r.delete(lock_key)
    else:
        raise Exception("Could not acquire lock")
```

The lock uses Redis's SET command with NX (only if not exists) and EX (expire) options. This ensures the lock is automatically released if the process crashes.

## Tradeoffs and considerations
- **Performance**: Locks add latency to operations
- **Complexity**: Need to handle lock acquisition failures
- **Deadlocks**: Poorly designed locks can cause deadlocks

## Key takeaways
- Use distributed locks for critical sections in distributed systems
- Always include timeout handling to prevent permanent locks
- Test thoroughly under concurrent load conditions
```
